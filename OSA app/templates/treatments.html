No Obstructive Sleep Apnea

  • No treatment is required for normal OSA, as it is not considered a medical condition.

Mild Obstructive Sleep Apnea

  • Lifestyle changes such as weight loss, regular exercise, avoiding alcohol and sedatives, and sleeping on your side can help to reduce the symptoms of mild OSA.
  • Using a mandibular advancement device (MAD) that can be custom-made by a dentist to help keep your airway open during sleep.

Moderate Obstructive Sleep Apnea

  • Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy is the most common treatment for moderate to severe OSA. It involves wearing a mask over your nose or mouth that delivers a continuous stream of air to keep your airway open while you sleep.
  • Bi-level positive airway pressure (BiPAP) therapy is similar to CPAP but delivers different pressures for inhaling and exhaling.

Severe Obstructive Sleep Apnea

  • In addition to CPAP or BiPAP therapy, other treatments for severe OSA may include surgery to remove excess tissue in the throat, or to reposition the jaw or tongue.
  • Another option is to use an implanted device that stimulates the nerves controlling the tongue to keep the airway open during sleep.
